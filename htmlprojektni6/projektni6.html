Timothy Berners-Lee: The Inventor of the World Wide Web

Timothy Berners-Lee is a British computer scientist who invented the World Wide Web.

Born on June 8, 1955, in London, Berners-Lee studied physics at Oxford University before becoming a software engineer.

In 1980, while working at CERN (the European Particle Physics Laboratory in Geneva), he first proposed the concept of a global system based on hypertext that would allow researchers to share information across the globe. He also developed a prototype named 'Enquire'.

In 1984, he returned to CERN, which was a major European Internet hub at the time. In 1989, he published a paper titled "Information Management: A Proposal", which combined hypertext with the Internet, creating a system for global information sharingâ€”this system would eventually be known as the World Wide Web.

Berners-Lee created the first web browser and editor. The first website, http://info.cern.ch, was launched on 6 August 1991, introducing users to the World Wide Web and providing an introduction to creating websites.

In 1994, he founded the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) and has served as its director since then. He also continues to work as a senior research scientist at MIT's Computer Science and Artificial Intelligence Laboratory (CSAIL).
